Productive Struggle: What a Missed Putt Taught Me About Writing
\
\

There is a specific feeling you get when you miss a putt you really should have made. Three feet out, dead straight, and somehow you yank it left. You can stand there and replay it in your head, or you can reset your stance and go again. I have been on both sides of that. On the golf course and at a desk. And the only version of me that actually got better is the one that lined back up.

\ \

Freshman year my writing was fine. Organized, had a thesis, checked the boxes. I would turn something in and feel done with it, like the assignment was just something to get off my plate. Nothing pushed back hard enough to make me think differently about what I was actually saying. I got decent grades and figured that meant I was doing alright. Looking back I think I was just comfortable, and comfortable is another word for stuck.

\ \

The thing about ESD is it does not let you stay there. Sophomore and junior year the assignments started demanding more. A real argument, not just a summary with an opinion at the end. I started getting papers back with actual comments on them, not just a grade but real feedback pointing at real problems. It stung sometimes. But I started reading the comments instead of just flipping to the grade and moving on. That was the first real shift, being open to the idea that the paper I thought was solid actually was not. Openness is not something you just naturally have. For me it got built through a lot of marked up drafts I did not want to look at.

\ \

Now I am in AP Lang and its honestly the most my writing has ever been pushed. Rhetorical analysis makes you slow down in a way that is genuinely uncomfortable. You cannot just say what an author is doing, you have to figure out why they did it, what effect it creates, what they were trying to make you feel. There are moments in this class where I honestly do not know what I am doing and I just have to sit with that until something clicks. That is discernment to me. Not having the answer right away but being willing to stay in the confusion long enough to find something real instead of grabbing whatever sounds decent first.

\ \

The clearest proof I have of all of this is the essay I wrote about golf. I wrote about anger and frustration and the way the game humbles you no matter how much you practice. About the 17th hole with my dad where I hit a perfect shot and watched it splash into the pond anyway. About how golf does not care about cause and effect the way school does. I wrote that golf mirrors me, it shows me things I don't always want to see, and I meant every word of it. That essay did not come from nowhere. It came from four years of being pushed, marked up, and forced to keep going until the voice on the page actually sounded like mine. You can see the difference between what I was doing freshman year and what I was able to do by the time I wrote that piece. That gap is what productive struggle looks like.

\ \

And that is the thing that gets lost when people skip the struggle, including reaching for AI to just do the work. I get it. AI is useful. It can help you brainstorm, give you a starting point, clean up a structure. But if you use it to hand you the finished product, you are robbing yourself of the only process that actually builds judgment. Discernment cannot be downloaded. You build it by writing the bad version yourself, sitting with the feedback, and doing it again. A clean AI draft with no friction in it teaches you nothing about your own thinking. A rough grade on something you genuinely fought through tells you exactly where to look next time.

\ \

Perseverance at ESD is not just grinding. It is staying open when something is hard instead of finding a way around it. Balancing a full AP load with lacrosse and everything that comes with senior year, there are weeks where I am not at my best and I know it. But I still show up and do the work myself. Because that is the point. Thriving here does not look like a perfect record. It looks like the willingness to line back up after the miss, stay open to being wrong, and trust that the struggle is building something real even when it does not feel like it.

\ \

Golf taught me that first. Four years of writing confirmed it.
